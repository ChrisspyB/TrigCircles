Sinusoids and Circles

Consider the unit circle. Starting from the positive x-axis and tracing the circle, plotting the y coordinate as a function of the polar angle t, we produce a sine wave of period 2pi i.e. y = sin(t).
#small_sin_trace
If were to instead start tracing 90 degrees later i.e. from the positive y-axis we see that the same procedure produces a cosine wave. This isn't surprising, given that cos(t) = sin(t+90).
#small_cos_trace
By varying the parameters of the circle, we can plot arbitrary sinusoidal functions Asin(t+α). Notice how varying the radius, trace speed and starting position affects the amplitude, frequency and phase-offset of the resulting sinusoid.

Fourier Series

From the study of Fourier Analysis, it turns out that any periodic function can be expressed as an infinite sum of sines and cosines, provided the function f(x).
  • has a finite period,
  • is single valued,
  • has an finite number of maxima/minima over one period,
  • has a finite number of discontinuities over one period,
  • encloses a finite area over one period.
Provided these conditions are met, the series will converge (non-uniformly) to the f(x) at all points were f(x) is continuous. At singularities, the series converges to the mid point of f(x) either side of the discontinuity.
These conditions are sufficient but not necessary: there exists (weird) functions with a convergent fourier series that do not satisfy these conditions.

It follows therefore that periodic functions can be approximated by truncating the series to include only the first few terms: a finite sum of sines and cosines and can therefore be visualised as a finite sum of circles.
